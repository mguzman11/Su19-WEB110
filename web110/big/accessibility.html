Accessibility

Accessibility in Web Design is essential for a well designed, functional, and user friendly web page. According to sitecontent.com, a web designing and consulting firm, ensuring that your website is accessible to all users, not only makes the site understandable to your users, but also to browsers and search engines, thus ensuring that users are directed to your site first. (sitecontent.com) Website accessibility may include, but certainly not limited to, alternate text descriptions for images, transcripts and/or closed captions for videos or voice recorded media, compatibility with screen readers, and keyboard input instead of relying on a mouse.

Accessibility in your website isn’t only smart business, but may be required by law. According to the World Wide Web Consortium, 22 countries and the European Union have adopted some sort of web accessibility policy or law pertaining to government websites. (W3C) In the United States, all federal websites are required to make their sites accessible to people with disabilities. (section508.gov) This means, if you are building a website for a federal government agency, it must comply with accessibility standards.

There are several ways to ensure that your website is accessible. One way is to use an accessibility site checker, like this one: https://www.powermapper.com/products/sortsite/checks/accessibility-checks/. This site checker compares your site’s accessibility to the WC3’s accessibility standards and the section 508 standards. However, some accessibility issues require a human judgement to determine it is accessible by all.

Knowing accessibility best practices is still necessary for building any website. Here are a few best practices: Do not rely on color as a navigational tool, all images should have an alt text and/or captions for the image, functionality should be accessible through a mouse and keyboard and be able to work with voice-control systems, provide transcripts when necessary, provide closed captioning for video, provide a way to skip navigation in your site, and consider 508 compliance testing. (usability.gov)

Designing websites that are accessible to all does not require much more work on the designer’s part and should always be implemented.
